NANDA Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Find answers to commonly asked questions about the NANDA protocol, its implementation, and use cases.

1. What is MCP, and how does NANDA relate?
  • Model Context Protocol (MCP) is a foundational protocol enabling structured communication between AI-driven software agents. Think of MCP as the common "language" agents use to exchange data and coordinate actions.
  • NANDA (Networked AI Agents in Decentralized Architecture) enhances MCP by providing critical infrastructure: verified identities, security standards, trusted registries, and marketplace capabilities. In other words, MCP handles how agents communicate, and NANDA ensures their interactions are secure, trusted, and effective across organizational boundaries.
2. Is NANDA centralized or decentralized?

NANDA operates as a decentralized network, hosted independently across global organizations. This decentralized model enhances resilience, openness, and fairness in digital interactions.

3. What specific challenges does NANDA address?

NANDA tackles significant barriers in multi-agent ecosystems, including:

  • Ensuring data privacy and security through identity verification.
  • Facilitating secure transactions and interactions.
  • Providing efficient agent discovery and reputation management.
  • Enabling coordinated collaboration across organizations without centralized control.
4. Who are the key beneficiaries of NANDA?
  • Developers and Researchers: Access robust frameworks to develop secure, decentralized AI applications quickly.
  • Enterprises: Streamline integration and automate complex business processes across multiple platforms securely.
  • End Users: Experience safe, intelligent automation of complex daily tasks.
5. What's a practical example of NANDA in action?

There are infinite use cases across sectors. For example, financial institutions often face challenges in detecting fraud due to data silos and privacy concerns. With NANDA, banks can deploy AI agents that collaborate across institutions to identify fraudulent patterns without sharing sensitive customer data. This decentralized approach enhances fraud detection capabilities while maintaining data privacy and compliance.

6. How easy is it for organizations to adopt NANDA?

Extremely accessible. Organizations can rapidly adapt existing APIs or services, often within hours, to become part of the secure and open NANDA ecosystem.

7. How can I start participating or contributing to NANDA?

Visit NANDA.mit.edu for detailed documentation, contribution guidelines, open-source repositories, and partnership opportunities. Join our global working groups, contribute to open-source developments, or deploy your services to leverage the decentralized future of AI.

NANDA vs Google A2A

This comparison highlights key differences between NANDA and Google's Agent-to-Agent (A2A) approach:

Aspect NANDA Google A2A
Control & Hosting Emphasizes decentralized controlâ€”anyone can run an agent, a registry, or a host locally or in the cloud Designed for enterprise deployment, assuming agents live in cloud environments or enterprise systems
Design Philosophy Built for open, user-sovereign ecosystems where developers retain control and can innovate outside large platforms Built for enterprise-grade interoperability, focused on structured workflows and integration with existing SaaS systems
Registry & Discovery Includes support for federated or decentralized registries, enabling local and community-driven networks Assumes a centralized or vendor-backed registry for agent capabilities and availability (e.g., Agent Cards)
Audience Geared toward researchers, protocol hackers, decentralized web developers, and open-source communities Targeted at enterprises, vendors, and managed service providers deploying large agent networks
Governance Model Community-governed and evolving via MIT's open research (based on MCP and decentralized agent principles) Google-led with backing from over 50 tech partners; direction set by Google Cloud and collaborators
Core Use Case Enabling decentralized agents to interact peer-to-peer across domains, including personal computing, local networks, and open ecosystems Streamlining cross-enterprise agent workflows like task delegation, ticketing, CRM integration, and supply chain ops

While both NANDA and Google A2A aim to enable agent communication, NANDA's focus on decentralization and open ecosystems makes it particularly valuable for applications requiring sovereignty, privacy, and cross-organizational collaboration without central control.
